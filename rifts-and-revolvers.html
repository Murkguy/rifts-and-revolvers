Chapter One

The Shattered Frontier

"The Rifts opened in '72. The railroad barons said it was God's gift. The machines that crawled out said otherwise. The Seers just smiled." — Overheard at the Dustfall Saloon, Dimension Seven

Rifts & Revolvers is set in the Shattered Frontier — a patchwork of overlapping dimensions where the American West collides with futures, pasts, and places that have no right to exist. Dimensional Rifts tear through reality without warning, depositing clockwork automatons, spell-slingers, and horrors into dusty frontier towns that already had plenty of their own problems.

Players take on the role of Frontier folk — gunslingers, healers, tricksters, brawlers, and wanderers — each navigating a world where two things are always true: the dice tell your story, and the cards change it.

The Three Layers

Every action in Rifts & Revolvers runs through three interlocking systems. Understanding how they divide their responsibilities is the key to the whole game.

Dice — The Story

Poker dice resolve what happens. Roll five dice, build the best poker hand you can across up to three rolls, and the result tells you whether you succeeded and by how much. Only roll when failure has a real consequence — if you can't describe what losing looks like before you roll, don't roll.

Cards — The Edge

A double deck of playing cards (104 cards) sits at the center of the table. Every player holds a secret five-card Persistent Hand dealt at session start. Cards don't tell the story — they bend it. Discard a card to fuel a reroll. Trigger a class ability. Fuel the GM's pressure engine. The cards are what you do when the dice aren't enough.

Chips — Your Status

Five colors of poker chips track your health, nerve, luck, wealth, and the dimensional debt accumulating against your soul. They sit in front of you and tell the table exactly what kind of shape you're in — without anyone rolling a thing.

Chapter Three

The Stack — Poker Chip Economy

"Everyone at the table puts something in. The house always takes its cut. The Rift doesn't care if you're broke — but it notices when you're flush."

Five colors of chips track your current state. They are physical objects that move across the table in real time. A standard poker set provides exactly these five colors — no additional tokens needed. Chips are gained, lost, and transferred between players. Running out of White means you're down. Running up on Black means something is coming for you.

W
White — Grit
Health · Stamina · Physical Resilience
Your body. Lose one per hit in a Duel or Shootout. At zero you are Downed. Restored by Rest scenes (+3) or Doctor abilities. Refreshes to your class maximum at session start.
Spend 1 White to shrug off a Grazed wound — narrate it as a clean miss. Spend 2 White to reenter a scene after being Downed, with 2 White remaining.
R
Red — Nerve
Courage · Morale · Will to Continue
Your nerve. Lost when you witness something terrible, fail a social gambit badly, or are publicly humiliated. At zero you Break — you cannot initiate any action next scene. Does not refresh at session start; must be earned back through play.
Spend 1 Red to attempt a Quick Draw without sacrificing your card hand. Spend 1 Red to resist a Charlatan or Seer compulsion effect.
B
Blue — Luck
Fortune · The Favor of the Rift
Pure luck — rare and not restored passively. Earned through great narration, risky plays that pay off, or GM award. The GM awards Blue chips when the table delivers a moment worth remembering.
Spend 1 Blue to reroll any single die after the final reveal — before consequences are applied. Spend 2 Blue to survive what should have killed you. Once per session: you are Downed but not dead.
G
Green — Coin
Money · Barter · Social Standing
Wealth and reputation — the same thing on the Frontier. The only chip that transfers freely between players. Spent on gear, bribes, information, and gambling antes. Won in poker hands and heists. Lost through bad deals and failed cons.
Spend 3 Green to hire a Grunt NPC for one scene. Spend 5 Green to obtain Veteran-tier information. Ante Green in any gambling scene — winner takes the pot.
✦
Black — Rift Debt
The Price · Void Marks · What You Owe
You gain these — they are a liability. Acquired by interacting with dimensional forces, using certain card abilities, and triggering Rift events. At 5 Black chips, the GM introduces a Rift Collector — an entity that arrives to collect. Black chips cannot be lost through rest or normal play. They must be shed.
Shed 1 Black by completing a Crossroads scene that costs you something real. Shed 3 Black by defeating your Rift Collector in a Duel. Never recover Black through rest — the debt doesn't sleep.

The GM's Fate Chips

The GM does not use the five player chip colors. Instead the GM holds a single pool of Fate Chips — a neutral color marker (coins, stones, or a sixth color if available). The GM starts each session with zero Fate Chips and builds them through play.

The GM Is the House

The GM does not roll dice against players. Instead, the GM sets stakes by placing Fate Chips in the center of the table. To attempt a consequential action, a player must match the Fate Chips with chips of their own — committing resources before the dice are thrown.

Fate Chips PlacedDifficultyRequired HandExample Stake
1 chipEasyTwo Pair or betterPicking a drunk's lock, bluffing a Grunt
2 chipsModerateThree of a KindDisarming a trap, persuading a Veteran NPC

The GM chooses which type of chip the player must match. Demanding White chips means physical cost. Demanding Red means nerve. Demanding Black means only the willing to acquire Rift Debt may attempt this.

How the GM Earns Fate Chips

Every time a player redraws cards (through a class ability or any mechanic that refills their Persistent Hand), the GM gains 1 Fate Chip. This is the closed loop at the heart of the game: players need better cards to win, but getting better cards feeds the GM.

The GM spends Fate Chips to redraw their own card hand (3 chips per full redraw) and to activate certain GM face card abilities.

Chapter Four

The Poker Dice System

All resolution uses five poker dice — each face showing 9, 10, J, Q, K, A. Build the best poker hand you can across up to three rolls by locking dice between throws. The hand ranking determines the outcome. Only roll when failure has a real consequence.

The Roll Sequence

1
Declare Stakes. Name what you're risking and what you want. The GM sets Fate Chips. You decide whether to match them or walk away.
2
Roll All Five. Throw your full pool. Assess your hand.
3
Lock & Reroll. Lock any dice — they stay face-up and don't reroll. Roll the rest. Repeat once more for a maximum of three rolls total.
4
Final Hand. Locked + final rolled dice form your hand. Compare to the required threshold or opponent's hand.
→
Narrate. Winner narrates first. Loser responds. In ties, both narrate simultaneously — chaos ensues.

Dice Modifiers — The Four Mechanics

These four mechanics can be accessed by any class, but what triggers them depends on your class and the cards you hold. The trigger is class-specific; the mechanic is universal.

LOCK — Pre-Roll Certainty

Declare one or more dice values before rolling. Place those dice face-up showing the declared value. They are locked from the start — you chose your outcome before chance had a word. Locked dice from this ability cannot be unlocked.

Naturally suits: Gunslinger (precision), Cogwright (preparation). Available to all classes through the right card ability or situation.

WILD DIE — Post-Roll Manipulation

After your final roll, nominate one die and set it to any face value you choose. Unlike a Lock, this happens after you see your results. You're fixing what went wrong, not preventing it.

Naturally suits: Brawler (chaotic adaptation), Charlatan (misdirection). Available to all through specific card triggers.

REROLL — Extra Chances

The standard sequence gives you three rolls. Discard a number card (2–10) from your Persistent Hand matching the suit of your current action to gain one additional reroll. Any matching-suit number card grants exactly one reroll regardless of its value — a 2 and a 10 both grant one reroll. You may do this multiple times per roll sequence by discarding multiple cards.

Face cards out of class cannot be discarded for rerolls — they must be passed left or held.

WILD CARD — Rift Exclusive

When a Wild Card is declared, one specific die value (chosen by the Seer or GM) becomes equal to Ace for the remainder of the scene. Every player's hand — and every NPC's — is affected simultaneously. A hand full of Threes suddenly beats a hand full of Kings.

Wild Card access is exclusive to the Seer and the GM. The Seer must discard a Diamond face card to declare it. The GM spends 2 Fate Chips. The Chaos Track gains 1. Wild Cards cannot be countered.

Locking Between Rolls

Standard Lock Rules

Between any two rolls, you may lock any number of dice by placing them face-up. Locked dice are not rerolled. You may unlock a die before your second roll only — once you begin your third roll, all locked dice are permanent. Locking all five dice ends your turn early with your current hand.

BUST — The Gambler's Risk

After your second roll, declare a Bust: discard all locked dice and roll all five fresh. If your new hand beats your discarded hand, gain 1 Blue chip — luck rewarded. If it doesn't, your opponent acts first automatically, and you lose 2 White chips from the physical or emotional whiplash. The Frontier punishes gambling that doesn't pay.

Chapter Five

The Persistent Hand — Your Secret Five

"Nobody said a word. The Cogwright slid one card left without looking up. The Charlatan palmed it, her expression unchanged. Somewhere across the table, the Gunslinger understood something had changed."

At session start, deal every player — and the GM — five cards from a shuffled double deck (104 cards). This is their Persistent Hand, held privately all session. Cards are spent, passed, and burned. They are never shown. They are never discussed. What you hold is yours alone until you choose otherwise.

The Three Card Laws

Law 1 — Secrecy

Never show your cards. Never describe them verbally. Not the suits, not the values, not how many of a kind. Meaningful glances are permitted. Sighing is permitted. Sliding a card toward someone you trust is permitted. Truth about your hand is not.

You may express a need — "someone pass a card to the Doctor" — but you may not describe what you're passing or why in mechanical terms.

Law 2 — The Pass

Once per scene, pass one card face-down to the player on your left only. They must accept it and add it to their hand. They then discard one card face-down to the bottom of the deck to return to five cards. They choose which to discard. You cannot pass right, across, or back. Passing is always optional — holding all session is a valid and lonely strategy.

Law 3 — The Discard Economy

Number cards (2–10) of your class suit: Discard to trigger a class ability (see Chapter Six) or to gain one reroll on a matching-suit action.
Number cards (2–10) out of class suit: Discard during a matching-suit action to gain one reroll. A 3 of Clubs is still one reroll for a non-Brawler when she's in a brawl.
Face cards (J/Q/K/A) of your class suit: Trigger powerful class abilities. Cannot be discarded for simple rerolls — they are worth too much.
Face cards out of class suit: Cannot be discarded for rerolls. Must be passed left or held for the Resonance mechanic. These are someone else's treasure.

Table Moment

Vera (Seer) is sitting on four Diamonds and an off-suit Seven. She needs one more Diamond to trigger her 5-Diamond class ability. She can't say so. She passes the Seven left to Cal (Gunslinger) — a useless card for her, neutral for him. Cal knows she passed something she didn't want. He has a Diamond in his hand. He passes it to Vera next scene. She doesn't know what's coming. When it lands, she has her five Diamonds. She doesn't react. She doesn't have to.

Recharge — Drawing Back to Five

You do not automatically redraw after spending cards. Your hand shrinks as you spend. Recharge is a class ability — some classes have it, some don't, some can grant it to teammates. When you recharge, you draw cards from the Frontier Deck until you hold five again. Every card you draw triggers 1 Fate Chip for the GM.

The Double Deck — Resonance

With 104 cards, the same card exists twice. If two players reveal or discard the same card simultaneously, Resonance triggers: both players' class effects fire at full strength regardless of their current suit majority, both gain 1 Blue chip, and the Chaos Track gains 1. The duplicate shouldn't exist. The Frontier notices.

Opening hand probability: ~34% chance of holding at least one face card of your class suit. ~10% chance of having suit majority (3+ cards of your suit) for a class trigger. The Pass mechanic exists precisely because you usually don't start with what you need.

Chapter Six

Class Abilities — What Your Cards Do

Class abilities trigger from cards in your Persistent Hand. Number cards (2–10) of your suit trigger scaled abilities based on the action context. Face cards trigger named abilities that are qualitatively different — not just stronger, but accessing angles number cards cannot reach.

All classes can access Rerolls, Locks, and Wild Dice through the right situation and cards. What differs is when and why each class can access them, and what their face cards specifically unlock.

♥ Hearts Classes

Doctor — Suit: Hearts | Honor +2

Core Ability — Treat: Discard any Hearts number card targeting an ally to restore White chips equal to the card's context tier: low cards (2–5) restore 1 White, mid cards (6–9) restore 2 White, 10 restores 3 White. The Doctor is the only class that restores White chips through card play outside of Rest scenes.

Recharge Ability — Field Medicine: After successfully treating an ally (restoring any White chips), the Doctor may draw 1 card from the Frontier Deck. This is the primary recharge trigger — helping others fills your hand.

Teammate Recharge: Discard a Hearts face card to allow one ally to draw cards back up to five. This costs the Doctor a powerful card. The GM gains Fate Chips equal to the cards drawn.

Cogwright — Suit: Clubs | Honor +2

Core Ability — Prepared: During any scene where the Cogwright has had time to prepare (not in immediate combat), discard any Clubs number card to Lock one die before rolling. The preparation was the work — the roll is just confirming it.

Recharge Ability — Salvage: After successfully modifying, repairing, or building something during a scene, draw 1 card. The work fills the mind.

Green Chip Recovery: Sell a working device or modification to gain Green chips equal to the scene complexity (GM determines: 1–4 chips).

Face CardNamed Ability

Brawler — Suit: Clubs | Notorious −2

Core Ability — Wild Swing: In any physical confrontation (Bar Brawl, Shootout, or physical Duel), discard any Clubs card to access a Wild Die — setting one die to any value after rolling. The Brawler doesn't aim. They adjust.

Recharge Ability — Standing: Win any physical exchange (any roll in a Bar Brawl or Shootout where your hand beats the opponent's) to recover 1 White chip and draw 1 card. Winning hurts less than losing.

Chapter Seven

The Rift — Reading Dimensional Signs

"The card came up Diamond. Not his class. Not his suit. But it was an Ace and the Rift was right there and he understood, for a moment, what the crackle in the air was saying."

Any character may attempt to read a dimensional Rift — to draw meaning from the tears in reality that scar the Frontier. But only certain people can reliably do so. The Rift doesn't care about your courage or your skill. It cares whether you can hear it.

Who Can Read the Rift

Seer: May attempt a Rift Reading with any Diamond card from their Persistent Hand. The full value range (2 through Ace) is available. This is the Seer's core identity.

All other classes: May only attempt a Rift Reading if they hold a face card of Diamonds (J♦, Q♦, K♦, or A♦) in their Persistent Hand. The face card represents an unusually strong moment of dimensional sensitivity — rare, unpredictable, and costly.

Card UsedWho Can UseReading Result
J♦AnyoneA fragment — an image, a word, a feeling. One vague true thing from the GM.
Q♦AnyoneA partial reading — a direction, a warning, an incomplete name. Useful but incomplete.
K♦AnyoneA clear vision — specific, actionable information about the Rift or what moves through it.
A♦AnyoneFull sight — complete information, but the Rift sees you back. Gain the answer AND 2 Black chips.
2♦–10♦Seer onlySame as J♦ for 2–5; same as Q♦ for 6–9; same as K♦ for 10.

Reading the Rift always requires discarding the card used — the insight burns the moment it's received. Non-Seer characters who attempt a reading also gain 1 Black chip regardless of card value. The Rift charges for unsanctioned access.

Chapter Eight

The Scale — Honor & Notoriety

Every character sits somewhere on a scale from −5 (deeply notorious) to +5 (deeply honorable). Zero is not neutral — it's stagnant. The Frontier rewards commitment to an identity. Players who hedge, who try to be everything, who act honorably when convenient and notoriously when profitable — they stagnate mechanically while committed players advance.

Spending XP

New class ability (within your current class): 10–15 XP depending on power tier. Early abilities cost 10. Advanced abilities cost 15.
Class transition: 20 XP plus a narrative threshold moment — a scene where the change is earned in fiction, not just on a character sheet.
Abilities are permanent. You don't lose old class abilities when you transition. The retired Bandit who became a Doctor still knows how to disrupt a roll and take from others. They just also know how to heal.

Class Transitions

To transition to a new class, your scale position must reach that class's home position or beyond. Transitioning from Bandit (−2) to Doctor (+2) requires your scale to reach +2 — a four-point journey through the stagnation zone where you earn only 3 XP per session. The stagnation is the transition cost. You are slow because you are changing.

Chapter Nine

Minigames — Scene Types

Everyone can participate in every scene using the base dice rules. Class mechanics are advantages within minigames — not gates to them. The exception: Rift readings require Diamond cards as described in Chapter Seven. Everything else is open to all.

Minigame One · Spades
High Noon — The Duel
"The street went quiet. The kind of quiet that isn't peaceful — the kind that's waiting."

A Duel is a structured one-on-one contest. Gunslingers and Bandits have the deepest mechanical roots here, but any character can duel. The difference is that a Gunslinger's cards make them terrifying at it.

1
Draw Phase. Each duelist rolls one die. Higher result chooses whether to go first or second. Ties re-roll. Going first means you lock dice without seeing your opponent's choices.
2
The Hand. Both parties roll their full three-roll sequence simultaneously, keeping locked dice hidden.
3
Showdown. Reveal simultaneously. Resolve using the hand ranking table.
4
Wound Track. Loser loses White chips equal to the rank difference between hands (min 1, max 4 per exchange).
↺
Repeat or Resolve. If both are standing, restart from Draw Phase. Red chips do not restore between rounds of the same Duel.

Wound Conditions

White Chips Lost (cumulative)ConditionMechanical Effect
1–2GrazedLose 1 card draw on your next recharge
3–4WingedRoll only 4 dice until treated
5–6BleedingRoll only 3 dice; no card abilities this scene
7+DownedOut of Duel. Opponent decides your fate.

The Quick Draw

Skip the Draw Phase and go first automatically. Cost: discard your highest Spades card from your Persistent Hand, or spend 1 Red chip if you have no Spades. Roll once, lock what you have, reveal. Win and you narrate a cinematic single shot. Lose by even one rank — take 3 White chip damage immediately. High risk. High drama.

The Gambit Card

Before any Duel, each duelist may draw one card from the Frontier Deck as a secret Gambit. Play it at one of three moments — "Before the draw" (add value as a bonus substitutable die result), "On the pull" (force opponent to unlock one locked die), or "At the showdown" (if it matches your class suit, flip the outcome for 2 Black chips). Face cards have named Gambit effects: Jacks distract, Queens read minds, Kings bluff, Aces add a sixth die.

Minigame Two · All Classes
Lead & Lightning — The Shootout
"Twelve guns, two sides, one street. The Cogwright dove behind a steam cart. The Drifter was already gone."

Group combat involving three or more active combatants. Unlike the structured Duel, a Shootout is spatial and chaotic — positioning matters, the Chaos Track climbs, and the Frontier can tear open at any moment.

Initiative — The Spread

Every combatant rolls one die. Highest goes first; ties act simultaneously. Each turn: take a Shot (roll against one target) or take Cover (gain defensive benefits but cannot attack this turn).

Cover Cards — The Position Game

At scene start the GM draws one card per two combatants (round up) and places them face-down as Cover Positions. Characters claim a position or stand in the open. Cards reveal when first attacked or abandoned.

Card ValueCover TypeBenefit
2–4Thin (barrel, horse, stall)Attacker rerolls one die. One-use — destroyed after first hit.
5–7Solid (wagon, wall, boulder)Attacker rolls 4 dice instead of 5.
8–10Heavy (vault door, locomotive)Attacker rolls 3 dice. Survives 3 hits before breaking.
J–QFortified (barricade, raised position)Attacker rolls 3 dice; no card abilities this turn.
KCommanding (high ground, rooftop)This character acts first regardless of initiative. Cannot be targeted at range while held.
APerfect (sealed room, dimensional fold)Cannot be targeted. Only engaged by someone who abandons their own cover to advance.

Suit of the Cover Card adds a twist: ♠ = shadow position (attack one enemy without being targetable back), ♣ = structural (double hit capacity), ♥ = exposed flank (second enemy may target for free), ♦ = Rift-adjacent (gain 1 Blue and 1 Black when revealed).

The Chaos Track

Starts at 0. Rises when: a Bust is declared (+1), a Wild Card is called (+1), Five of a Kind is rolled (+1), or a Seer or Drifter face card triggers a dimensional effect (+1 or +2 as noted). At Chaos 5, a Rift Tear erupts.

Minigame Three · Clubs
Last Call — The Bar Brawl
"Nobody drew a gun. That's how you knew it was serious."

Close-quarters chaos. No cover cards — too close for that. No initiative order — everything happens simultaneously and messy. The Brawler is at home here. Everyone else is just trying to survive the furniture.

Simultaneous Resolution

In a Bar Brawl, all participants declare their target and play one card from their Persistent Hand face-down simultaneously. All cards reveal at once. Higher card value in the exchange wins it — but certain combinations create results nobody planned for.

The Card Exchange

The Brawler's Advantage

In a Bar Brawl, the Brawler's low cards are not weak. Any Clubs card (2–10) played by a Brawler in a Bar Brawl also grants them a Wild Die — they set one die to any value on their next dice roll, no discard required. The chaos of the fight is their natural habitat. A 3 of Clubs in a Brawler's hand during a bar fight is worth more than a King of Spades in anyone else's.

Escalation

Bar Brawls don't stay contained. After each round, roll one die. On a 9, someone draws a weapon — the Bar Brawl becomes a Shootout. On an A, someone goes through a window and the scene ends with them outside on the street. On anything else, it continues. The Chaos Track applies to Bar Brawls — but escalates faster: starts at 2, not 0.

Minigame Four · Hearts
Words Like Weapons — The Confrontation
"She didn't threaten him. She didn't need to. She just described, very quietly, what would happen next."

Social pressure, persuasion, seduction, intimidation, negotiation. The Charlatan and Seer have the deepest mechanics here, but a Gunslinger's reputation and a Brawler's presence are real factors in social scenes too.

Confrontation Stakes

The GM sets Fate Chips as usual. The type of chip demanded signals the nature of the confrontation: Red chips mean it costs someone nerve to proceed, Green chips mean something material is at stake, White chips mean this confrontation has become physically dangerous.

The winning hand determines outcome quality exactly as in combat — a Full House wins decisively, a One Pair barely gets there.

The Charlatan's Stage

In any Confrontation where the Charlatan has established a false identity or working con, their Hearts cards cost nothing to trigger — no discard required. They are in their element. The cards flow naturally. This does not apply if the Charlatan is operating as themselves.

Romance — A Special Confrontation

Romance uses the same dice but carries emotional weight rather than damage. Both parties roll. The hand ranking describes the quality of the moment, not a winner. Five of a Kind creates a Bond. High Card creates a misunderstanding that deepens the relationship anyway. Both parties gain 1 Red chip on any roll of Three of a Kind or better — the moment restores nerve.

Minigame Five · Clubs & Diamonds
The Workshop — Building & Sabotage
"The Cogwright didn't call it a bomb. She called it a 'persuasion device.'"

Construction, modification, sabotage, and route-finding. The Cogwright builds things that change the physical world permanently. The Drifter navigates routes that shouldn't exist. Both use this space — one with grease and gears, the other with instinct and Rift-sense.

Chapter Ten

Running the Frontier — GM Tools

The GM's Hand

The GM holds five cards like every player. The GM's hand is not class-specific — they access abilities across all suits. Their cards are spent on narrative complications, escalations, and reversals. The GM's face cards do not trigger class abilities — they alter the scene structure itself.

GM Face Card Effects

CardEffect
Any JackComplication: An unexpected element enters the scene. Not hostile necessarily — just complicating. The GM narrates what arrives and why it changes things.
Any QueenEscalation: Chaos Track +1. One permanent element of the scene changes — a structural detail collapses, a secret is exposed, a door locks.
Any KingThe Other Player: A new named NPC enters with their own agenda conflicting with at least one character's goal. They are not the GM's puppet — they have their own scale position and motivations.
Any AceThe World Speaks: Every character experiences a vision, memory, or dimensional bleed simultaneously. No mechanical effect — pure story moment. No card discard required if it serves the narrative.
JokerThe Frontier Decides: Pass the card to the table. Players vote on the complication. Majority rules. The GM cannot veto.

The Fate Cut

At the start of each scene, the GM secretly draws one card and holds it face-down. This is the hidden shape of what the scene wants to become. Reveal it whenever the table needs momentum — when players feel too comfortable, when the scene stalls, or in response to a Joker. Unused Fate Cuts are discarded. The Frontier moves on.
